Abstract

Quantum computing, based on the principles of quantum physics, promises significant gains in computational power. However, it introduces new challenges, particularly for program verification. Classical verification techniques do not apply directly to quantum computing. This is mainly due to the use of quantum memory (non-standard information unit) and its measurement operation, which is the only way to retrieve information from a quantum bit and introduces a probabilistic aspect.

In this thesis, we focus on the circuit-based quantum computing model and a practical aspect of it: circuit transformations. A quantum circuit can be purely quantum (unitary circuit) or include classical control and measurement (hybrid circuit). Circuit transformations occur throughout the quantum computation process, whether compiling a circuit described in a high-level language to a low-level language, optimising a circuit for specific hardware (transpilation), changing the computation model, or transferring a quantum state (teleportation).

Each of these transformations can alter the circuit's behaviour. Therefore, tools are needed to verify that the behaviour is preserved. However, current tools are mostly limited to the unitary case and can only verify transformations that do not change the circuit type: a unitary circuit is transformed into another unitary circuit. Tools that support hybrid circuits are generally limited to hybridisation, where a unitary circuit is transformed into a hybrid circuit. The real challenge arises with the introduction of auxiliary wires, such as ancillas or discards, because these auxiliary wires must not affect the others.

Several techniques can verify a circuit: simulation, symbolic execution, and proof. They differ in their degree of automation and scalability. Simulation, while automatable, does not scale well. Symbolic execution can also be automated and scales better. Proof scales well but is difficult to automate. We therefore combine proof and symbolic execution: the former certifies a compilation pass from a high-level language to a low-level language, while the latter supports a tool for verifying the equivalence of hybrid circuits with discard.

To assist us in the proof process, we utilise the semi-automatic proof tool Qbricks, which is capable of proving parametrised unitary circuits based on path sums. This formalism provides a compact representation of circuits suitable for encoding.

As a prerequisite for developing our verification tool for hybrid circuits with discard, we define partial equivalence relations for unitary circuits and a mechanism to extend unitary verification tools to hybrid circuits. The combination of these two components enables the handling of hybrid circuits with discard.

To evaluate our approach, we implement our equivalence relations and our mechanism for extending tools limited to unitary circuits in SQbricks. Our results show that unitary tools extended to the hybrid case without discard substantially outperform the state of the art. They also show that adding a separation test at the unitary level allows us to handle much larger classes.
